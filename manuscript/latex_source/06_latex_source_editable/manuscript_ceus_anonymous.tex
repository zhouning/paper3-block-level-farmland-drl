\begin{abstract}
Real cadastral land-consolidation problems require decision support that handles irregular parcels, sparse adjacencies, heterogeneous areas, and policy thresholds for contiguous operating fields. We propose a block-level spatial abstraction for farmland consolidation planning. Swappable farmland and forest parcels are aggregated into spatially coherent consolidation blocks using barrier-aware segmentation and constrained clustering. A Maskable PPO scheduler allocates a limited consolidation budget across blocks, while a connectivity-aware greedy engine executes parcel-level farmland--forest exchanges within selected blocks. The framework follows a macroscopic planning, microscopic execution design and treats \textit{baimu fang} formation (100-mu consolidated fields) as a threshold-based consolidated-field metric evaluated with slope and contiguity. Experiments on three townships in Bishan District, Chongqing, using 3,607--12,950 Third National Land Survey parcels and 78--338 constructed blocks, show stable convergence across 15 seed-township combinations. The clearest scheduler-specific evidence occurs on the medium township: under the same within-block engine and reward, DRL is the only tested block-selection rule, including Reward-Greedy and finite-depth lookahead checks, to achieve positive \textit{baimu fang} area growth ($+12.9$\,ha versus negative baselines). On the small and large townships, reward-aware greedy planning remains competitive or stronger on several metrics. The contribution is therefore a scalable planning layer and evidence that learned scheduling can add value under specific multi-objective tension, rather than a general claim that reinforcement learning dominates heuristics. Because the experiments use one district and parcel-count-conserving rather than area-balanced swaps, the framework is best interpreted as a pre-screening and scenario-exploration tool.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Farmland consolidation reorganizes fragmented agricultural parcels into contiguous, productive blocks. In China, high-standard farmland policy defines operational field-block targets through standards such as GB/T~30600-2022 \citep{gbt30600_2022} and national construction plans \citep{moa2021plan}. One common planning unit is the \textit{baimu fang}, a 100-mu consolidated field ($\geq$6.67\,ha of contiguous farmland). In international terms, this is a threshold-based consolidated operating-field metric. In the mountainous regions of southwest China, where steep slopes, fragmented parcels, and interspersed forest are common, deciding which farmland and forest parcels to exchange is a large combinatorial problem.

Deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has been useful for sequential spatial planning, but its value for cadastral farmland consolidation is not obvious. Real parcels differ from grid cells in area, shape, adjacency, and barrier structure. These properties can make individual farmland--forest swaps nearly independent: large parcels dominate slope, sparse Queen contiguity limits neighborhood effects, and roads or waterways split the swappable graph into small components. A parcel-level MDP can therefore collapse toward a one-step ranking problem. We treat this as a design diagnosis, not a proof that all parcel-level DRL must fail.

We propose a \textbf{block-level spatial abstraction} that asks the agent to allocate a fixed consolidation budget across spatial units rather than individual parcels. Selecting one block consumes budget that cannot be used elsewhere, and swaps inside a block can affect adjacent blocks through township-wide contiguity and \textit{baimu fang} metrics. The framework has three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Spatial restructuring}: Thousands of irregular parcels are aggregated into tens to hundreds of spatially coherent \textit{consolidation blocks} using a hybrid method combining natural barrier segmentation (roads, waterways, construction land) with constrained agglomerative clustering.

\item \textbf{Block-level MDP}: The optimization is formulated as a Markov Decision Process where the agent selects which block to invest consolidation resources in at each step, observing per-block characteristics and global state metrics.

\item \textbf{Connectivity-aware execution}: Within each selected block, a deterministic greedy engine executes farmland--forest swaps using a connectivity-aware scoring function that preferentially removes steep, isolated farmland and converts gentle, farmland-adjacent forest---creating spatial spillover effects across block boundaries.
\end{enumerate}

This ``\textit{macroscopic planning, microscopic execution}'' design mirrors consolidation governance: planners allocate quotas across project areas, while local implementers execute parcel-level conversions. Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the architecture.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{paper3_framework_diagram.png}
\caption{Block-level planning framework. The DRL scheduler allocates budget across blocks, and a deterministic greedy engine executes parcel-level swaps within selected blocks.}
\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

We evaluate the framework on three townships with 3{,}607--12{,}950 parcels and 78--338 constructed blocks. Five block-selection baselines share the same within-block engine, including a one-step Reward-Greedy baseline that uses the same reward as DRL. We also run exact depth-two and pruned depth-three lookahead checks on Township~B, where the key scheduler gap appears. The principal findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The block-level MDP converges in all 15 seed--township combinations under identical hyperparameters.

\item The trained policies show township-specific profiles: balanced on Township~A, \textit{baimu fang}-area preserving on Township~B, and count-focused on Township~C.

\item On Township~B, DRL is the only tested rule to achieve positive \textit{baimu fang} area ($+12.9$\,ha versus $-101.9$, $-108.8$, and $-103.8$\,ha for Reward-Greedy, exact depth-two lookahead, and pruned depth-three lookahead). This is the clearest evidence for a scheduler-specific advantage.

\item On Townships~A and C, Reward-Greedy is competitive with or stronger than DRL on several metrics. The contribution is therefore block-level abstraction with a learnable scheduler, not a general claim that RL dominates heuristics.

\item Across seeds, the trained policies are stable, with reward coefficient of variation below 1\% on Township~C.
\end{enumerate}

These results support spatial abstraction as a practical design choice for scaling sequential optimization in farmland consolidation, while leaving parcel-level DRL, longer-horizon search, and area-balanced training as robustness priorities.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Farmland consolidation optimization}

Farmland consolidation has been studied with multi-objective evolutionary algorithms \citep{deb2002fast,cao2011sustainable,cao2012spatial}, hybrid genetic or game-theoretic methods \citep{liu2012multi}, ant-colony and cellular-automata couplings \citep{li2011coupling}, and integer programming \citep{aerts2003using}. These approaches are useful for land-use allocation, but most treat optimization as a static assignment problem. They rarely model the sequential allocation of limited consolidation effort across candidate project areas.

Chinese high-standard farmland policy emphasizes operational blocks such as \textit{baimu fang}. GB/T~30600-2022 specifies field size, slope, contiguity, access, and irrigation requirements \citep{gbt30600_2022}, while national plans set quantitative construction targets \citep{moa2021plan}. Existing optimization studies have not fully linked parcel exchange decisions to explicit consolidated-field formation, although land consolidation can improve agricultural productivity \citep{wu2005consolidation}.

\subsection{DRL for spatial optimization}

DRL has been applied to urban spatial planning \citep{zheng2023spatial}, urban generation \citep{wang2021deep}, and ecological connectivity planning \citep{equihua2024conservation}. These studies show that learned policies can support spatial configuration tasks, but they do not address rural cadastral consolidation where irregular parcels, hard land-type constraints, and policy-defined field-block targets interact. The gap addressed here is the combination of block-level sequential allocation, deterministic parcel-level execution, and \textit{baimu fang} metrics on real cadastral data.

\subsection{Hierarchical and multi-scale optimization}

Spatial optimization can be decomposed across scales. Hierarchical reinforcement learning decomposes tasks into sub-goals \citep{barto2003recent}, and spatial planning studies have coupled optimization scales through cellular automata \citep{li2011coupling}. Our design uses a simpler split: the agent chooses \textit{where} to invest, and a deterministic engine decides \textit{how} to execute parcel swaps.

\subsection{Multi-objective and constrained reinforcement learning}

The reward combines slope, contiguity, \textit{baimu fang} area, and \textit{baimu fang} count as a weighted scalar. This gives one policy-relevant operating point rather than a complete Pareto-conditioned policy. More formal multi-objective or constrained MDP formulations remain possible; here, the scalar reward keeps the planner interpretable and allows direct comparison with reward-aware greedy baselines.

\subsection{Spatial aggregation and constrained regionalization}

Block construction is related to constrained regionalization and zoning in GIScience. Methods such as SKATER, REDCAP, and constrained agglomerative clustering produce contiguous regions under adjacency constraints. We use a natural-barrier graph, with roads, waterways, and construction land excluded, so the resulting blocks have a physical planning interpretation rather than only a statistical one.

\paragraph{Cadastral data.} Third National Land Survey parcels for Bishan District were classified by DLBM code as farmland (011--013), forest (031--033), or other. \paragraph{Geometry and terrain.} Polygons were repaired, filtered for valid land-use codes, and re-projected to EPSG:32648. \section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

The farmland consolidation optimization is formulated as a tri-objective combinatorial problem. Given a set of parcels $\mathcal{P} = \{p_1, \ldots, p_M\}$ with land-use types $l_i \in \{\text{farmland}, \text{forest}, \text{other}\}$, slope values $s_i$, and areas $a_i$, the goal is to find a land-use assignment $\mathbf{l}^*$ that simultaneously:
\begin{align}
\text{Minimize:} \quad \bar{S} &= \frac{\sum_{i: l_i = F} s_i \cdot a_i}{\sum_{i: l_i = F} a_i} \quad \text{(area-weighted mean farmland slope)} \label{eq:slope} \\
\text{Maximize:} \quad \bar{C} &= \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_F|} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{P}_F} \frac{|\{j \in \mathcal{N}(i): l_j = F\}|}{|\mathcal{N}(i)|} \quad \text{(farmland contiguity)} \label{eq:cont} \\
\text{Maximize:} \quad B &= \sum_{k=1}^{K} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\text{area}(\mathcal{C}_k) \geq A_{\text{baimu}}\right] \quad \text{(\textit{baimu fang} count)} \label{eq:baimu}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{N}(i)$ is the Queen-contiguity neighborhood, $\mathcal{C}_k$ are farmland connected components, and $A_{\text{baimu}}=66{,}700$\,m$^2$. The optimization uses a 100 paired-swap budget and conserves farmland parcel count. Eq.~\ref{eq:baimu} is discrete and globally computed, so one parcel conversion can push a near-threshold connected component across the policy boundary.

\subsection{Block-level spatial abstraction}
\label{sec:blocks}

The key methodological step is aggregating parcels into \textit{consolidation blocks}, which reduce the action space, create budget competition among spatial units, and align decisions with project-scale planning.

\subsubsection{Hybrid barrier segmentation and clustering}

Block construction has five steps. We first extract farmland and forest parcels as swappable candidates, then build a Queen-contiguity graph after excluding roads, waterways, construction land, and other fixed barriers. Breadth-first search identifies barrier-delimited connected components. Components with more than 30 parcels are subdivided by Ward-linkage agglomerative clustering with a spatial connectivity constraint, targeting approximately 20 parcels per block. Blocks with fewer than 3 parcels or less than 0.5\,ha are removed. Table~\ref{tab:block_stats} and Fig.~\ref{fig:block_construction} summarize the result.

\subsection{Block-level Markov Decision Process}
\label{sec:mdp}

The optimization is a finite-horizon MDP $(\mathcal{S},\mathcal{A},\mathcal{T},R,H)$ in which each action selects one block for investment.

\subsubsection{State space}

At step $t$, the state contains per-block features $\mathbf{X}_t\in\mathbb{R}^{N_b\times17}$ and a global vector $\mathbf{g}_t\in\mathbb{R}^{9}$. The block features describe slope distribution, area, farmland/forest counts and ratios, within-block contiguity, compactness, neighboring-block context, remaining budget, and episode progress. The global vector records township-level farmland and forest slope, contiguity, farmland-count deviation, budget use, progress, current \textit{baimu fang} count and area, and count change. The raw state dimensions are 1{,}335, 2{,}253, and 5{,}755 for Townships A--C; the shared-scorer policy keeps the number of learnable parameters independent of $N_b$.

\subsubsection{Action space}

At each step, the agent selects one block $a_t \in \{1, 2, \ldots, N_b\}$. An action mask $\mathbf{m}_t \in \{0, 1\}^{N_b}$ ensures that only blocks containing both \textit{remaining unused} farmland and forest parcels are available:
\begin{equation}
m_t(b) = \mathbb{1}\!\left[\,n_F^{(b,\text{unused})}(t) > 0 \;\wedge\; n_L^{(b,\text{unused})}(t) > 0\,\right]
\end{equation}
where $n_F^{(b,\text{unused})}$ and $n_L^{(b,\text{unused})}$ count unused farmland and forest parcels. A parcel-level \texttt{swapped} mask prevents repeated use of the same parcel, so the 100-swap budget corresponds to distinct paired exchanges.

\subsubsection{Transition function: connectivity-aware greedy engine}
\label{sec:greedy_engine}

Upon selecting block $b$, the environment executes up to $\kappa=5$ farmland--forest swaps within that block.

\textbf{Farmland removal scoring.} For each farmland parcel $i$ in block $b$:
\begin{equation}
\text{score}_{\text{remove}}(i) = s_i - \delta \cdot |\{j \in \mathcal{N}(i) : l_j = F\}|
\label{eq:score_remove}
\end{equation}
where $\delta=0.5$ penalizes removal of well-connected farmland.

\textbf{Forest conversion scoring.} For each forest parcel $j$ in block $b$:
\begin{equation}
\text{score}_{\text{convert}}(j) = s_j - \gamma \cdot |\{k \in \mathcal{N}(j) : l_k = F\}|
\label{eq:score_convert}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma=1.0$ rewards forest parcels adjacent to farmland because the lowest score is converted. The 2:1 asymmetry between $\gamma$ and $\delta$ encodes an active-consolidation bias: fill gaps near existing farmland while discouraging destruction of well-connected fields. These constants are fixed for all townships and baselines.

The farmland parcel with the highest $\text{score}_{\text{remove}}$ and the forest parcel with the lowest $\text{score}_{\text{convert}}$ are swapped, provided that the removed farmland's slope exceeds the converted forest's slope (otherwise the swap would worsen the mean farmland slope, and it is skipped). This process repeats $\kappa$ times or until no improving swaps remain.

\textbf{Spatial spillover.} Contiguity and \textit{baimu fang} metrics are computed on the full township graph, so a swap at a block boundary can change scores and connected components in adjacent blocks. This creates inter-step dependencies that a block-level scheduler can exploit.

\subsubsection{Reward function}

The reward combines four normalized improvement channels:
\begin{equation}
r_t \;=\; w_S \cdot \widetilde{\Delta S}_t \;+\; w_C \cdot \widetilde{\Delta C}_t \;+\; w_B \cdot \widetilde{\Delta A}^{B}_t \;+\; b_B \cdot \Delta n^{B,+}_t
\label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where, denoting the area-weighted mean farmland slope by $\bar{S}_t$, the contiguity index by $\bar{C}_t$, the total \textit{baimu fang} area by $A^{B}_t$, the \textit{baimu fang} count by $n^{B}_t$, and the values at $t{=}0$ by $\bar{S}_0,\bar{C}_0,A^{F}_0$ (initial total farmland area):
\begin{align}
\widetilde{\Delta S}_t &= \frac{\bar{S}_{t-1} - \bar{S}_t}{|\bar{S}_0| + \varepsilon}, &
\widetilde{\Delta C}_t &= \frac{\bar{C}_t - \bar{C}_{t-1}}{|\bar{C}_0| + \varepsilon}, \\
\widetilde{\Delta A}^{B}_t &= \frac{A^{B}_t - A^{B}_{t-1}}{A^{F}_0 + \varepsilon}, &
\Delta n^{B,+}_t &= \max\!\left(0,\; n^{B}_t - n^{B}_{t-1}\right).
\end{align}
The weights are $w_S=2000$, $w_C=500$, $w_B=500$, and $b_B=20$ for all townships and seeds. A $-1.0$ penalty discourages selecting blocks that yield no completed swaps. The engine exactly conserves farmland parcel count, but not farmland area; area-balance implications are audited in Section~\ref{sec:area_drift} and discussed as a limitation.

\subsubsection{Episode structure}

Each episode has $H=20$ steps and $\kappa=5$ swaps per selected block, for a 100-swap budget. This setting balances within-block impact against enough macroscopic decisions for sequencing. A sensitivity check over $\kappa\in\{1,3,5,10,20\}$ is reported in Section~\ref{sec:kappa_ablation}. The discount factor is $\gamma_{\text{RL}}=0.99$.

\subsection{Baimu fang computation}
\label{sec:baimu}

\textit{Baimu fang} identification is performed after each step by breadth-first search on the full township farmland graph. Each connected component whose area is at least $66{,}700$\,m$^2$ is counted, and both count and total qualifying area are returned. The largest township requires approximately 1.3\,ms per call.

\subsection{Policy network architecture}
\label{sec:policy}

The \textit{BlockScoringPolicy} evaluates each block with shared weights, so learnable parameters do not depend on $N_b$. It has a scorer and a value network.

\textbf{Scorer network.} For each block, the 17 local features are concatenated with the 9 global features and mapped to a scalar logit:
\begin{equation}
\text{logit}_b = \text{Scorer}\!\left([\mathbf{x}_b;\, \mathbf{g}_t]\right) \in \mathbb{R}, \quad \text{Scorer}: \mathbb{R}^{26} \to \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}
implemented as a $26\to128\to64\to1$ MLP with $\tanh$ activations. The $N_b$ logits are passed through a masked softmax:
\begin{equation}
\pi(a_t = b \mid \mathbf{s}_t) \;=\; \frac{\exp(\text{logit}_b)\,\mathbb{1}[m_t(b)=1]}{\sum_{b'=1}^{N_b}\exp(\text{logit}_{b'})\,\mathbb{1}[m_t(b')=1]}.
\end{equation}
Each township is trained independently, but the architecture itself is dimension-invariant.

\textbf{Value network.} The state value depends only on global features:
\begin{equation}
V(\mathbf{s}_t) = \text{Value}(\mathbf{g}_t) \in \mathbb{R}, \quad \text{Value}: \mathbb{R}^{9} \to \mathbb{R}
\end{equation}
implemented as a $9\to64\to32\to1$ MLP. The model has 14{,}530 parameters and per-state inference is under 1\,ms on the largest township. We use orthogonal initialization \citep{saxe2014exact}, with gain 0.01 for the scorer and 1.0 for the value network.

\subsection{Baseline methods}
\label{sec:baselines}

All baselines use the same 20-step, $\kappa=5$, 100-swap block environment and the same within-block greedy engine. They differ only in block selection: Greedy-Global chooses the block with the largest one-step slope improvement; Greedy-Sequential follows blocks sorted by initial farmland slope; Random-Block samples valid blocks uniformly; Round-Robin cycles through blocks; Reward-Greedy simulates every unmasked block and selects the largest one-step multi-objective reward under Eq.~\ref{eq:reward}. We also run Township~B lookahead checks using the same reward and transition model: exact depth two and pruned depth three with beam width 8.

\subsection{Evaluation}

Trained models are evaluated with deterministic inference: at each step, the trained policy selects the block with the highest logit score (argmax over unmasked blocks). Each evaluation produces a single deterministic trajectory. We report slope change (\%), contiguity change, \textit{baimu fang} count change, \textit{baimu fang} area change (ha), and budget utilization across all 5 seeds. Because the current swap engine conserves parcel count rather than total farmland area, these metrics are interpreted as scenario-screening indicators rather than legally area-balanced implementation outcomes; the area-balance implication is revisited in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Farmland-area balance audit}
\label{sec:area_drift}

The engine conserves farmland parcel count, not farmland area. We therefore audited final farmland-area drift for every method (Table~\ref{tab:area_drift}).

\input{paper3_area_drift_table.tex}

DRL has small mean area drift on all townships ($+0.13$--$+0.28\%$), so its reported gains are not driven by large farmland expansion. However, some baselines drift more, and Reward-Greedy on Township~B loses both farmland area and \textit{baimu fang} area. The present implementation is therefore a screening tool; deployment requires area-tolerant or exact area-balanced transitions.

\subsection{Area-tolerance transition stress test}
\label{sec:area_tolerance}

We also ran a Township~B stress test with a transition engine that enforces cumulative farmland-area tolerance and accepts only slope-improving paired swaps. The DRL rows replay trained block histories under the modified transition; they are not retrained policies.

\input{paper3_area_tolerance_check_table.tex}

Under area tolerance, Reward-Greedy remains negative on \textit{baimu fang} area, and replayed DRL histories also become negative. This does not invalidate the count-conserving experiment, but it shows that operational use must retrain and evaluate under the deployment transition rule.

\subsection{Sensitivity to the swaps-per-step parameter \texorpdfstring{$\kappa$}{kappa}}
\label{sec:kappa_ablation}

The swaps-per-step parameter $\kappa$ controls the trade-off between many small block decisions and few large ones. We retrained Township~A for $\kappa\in\{1,3,5,10,20\}$ with a fixed 100-swap budget and 50{,}000 timesteps per setting (Table~\ref{tab:kappa_ablation}).

\input{paper3_kappa_ablation_table.tex}

The response is non-monotone. The extremes fail: $\kappa=1$ commits only $8/100$ swaps, while $\kappa=20$ leaves too few macro-decisions. The middle range performs best, with $\kappa=5$ giving the highest reward and $\kappa=10$ giving the largest \textit{baimu fang} area on Township~A. The default is therefore balanced, not universally optimal.


% ============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Why block-level abstraction can expose a useful scheduling problem}

Block-level abstraction can expose scheduling value because it adds three structures that individual parcel swaps often lack. First, the 100-swap budget creates resource competition across blocks. Second, the connectivity-aware engine creates spatial spillover across block boundaries. Third, the 6.67\,ha \textit{baimu fang} threshold makes some block choices discontinuously valuable. These mechanisms explain why a learned scheduler can help in selected cases.

The claim remains bounded. The results show that this block-level formulation is trainable and interpretable, and that the Township~B advantage is not reproduced by one-step Reward-Greedy, exact depth-two search, or pruned depth-three search. They do not prove that RL is uniquely necessary. Direct parcel-level DRL, value-guided rollouts, and longer-horizon search remain important comparisons.

\subsection{The ``invest-then-recover'' strategy on Township C}

Township~C illustrates objective switching. In the first ten steps, the agent accepts an early slope penalty to push near-threshold clusters across the \textit{baimu fang} threshold. In the remaining steps, it preserves the five new fields and recovers slope to a final $-0.32\%$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:trajectory_C}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{paper3_trajectory_C.png}
\caption{Township C trajectory of the DRL agent. The policy first forms five new \textit{baimu fang}, then recovers slope while holding the count fixed.}
\label{fig:trajectory_C}
\end{figure}

This pattern is not unique evidence for DRL, because Reward-Greedy forms six new fields on Township~C. It is useful mainly as an interpretable example of how block-level planning can shift between objectives within one episode.

\subsection{Policy implications}

The policy implication is decision support rather than automatic prescription. The block-level action resembles how planners allocate consolidation effort across project areas, and threshold-based metrics translate policy goals into computable objectives. However, cadastral parcels carry contracted management rights, and cross-ownership exchanges require negotiation and compensation \citep{wu2005consolidation}. The framework should therefore screen physical scenarios that planners can evaluate, not dictate implementable land adjustments. Administrative or cooperative boundaries could be added as hard barriers in block construction to reduce social transaction costs.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations remain. First, parcel-count conservation is not area conservation. The DRL area-drift audit is small under the tested setting, but the area-tolerance stress test shows that deployment should train directly under an area-tolerant or exact area-balanced transition. Second, the study lacks a direct parcel-level DRL baseline and exhaustive long-horizon search; the reported finite-depth checks strengthen but do not close the planning-baseline space. Third, \textit{baimu fang} identification is topological and does not yet enforce engineering shape requirements. Fourth, DRL does not dominate slope-only baselines, and the preferred reward weights depend on policy priorities. Fifth, block construction, $(\gamma,\delta)$, and $\kappa$ require broader sensitivity analysis. Finally, all townships are in one district, deterministic baselines lack uncertainty estimates, and DRL training is more costly than greedy planning. These boundaries support interpreting the framework as scalable scenario screening rather than a ready operational planner.


% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
% ============================================================================

This paper proposed a block-level spatial abstraction for DRL-based farmland consolidation planning. Across three townships, the block-level MDP converged reliably and produced stable township-specific strategies. The strongest scheduler-specific result occurs on Township~B, where DRL is the only tested method with positive \textit{baimu fang} area growth while Reward-Greedy and finite-depth lookahead remain negative. On Townships~A and C, however, Reward-Greedy is competitive or stronger on several metrics, showing that the main contribution is not a blanket claim that RL beats heuristics.

The defensible contribution is the combination of block-level abstraction, connectivity-aware within-block execution, and a learnable scheduler for cases with multi-objective tension. Because the current implementation conserves parcel count rather than exact farmland area and uses a topological \textit{baimu fang} definition, it should be read as a scenario-screening tool. Future work should prioritize area-balanced retraining, parcel-level and longer-horizon baselines, block-granularity sensitivity, and cross-district validation.
